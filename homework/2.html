Although it was one of the first concepts introduced, the idea and controversy of free will is something that caught and maintained my interest through the entirety of this course. Branching off this comes the concept of AI or “Artificial Intelligence”, which is the concept of a computer or robot being able to perform tasks as a human does due to them having human intelligence and perception of situations.

As knowledge of technology and breakthroughs in technological advancements increases, the idea of Artificial Intelligence grows every passing day. People are constantly finding ways of creating new things that can perform tasks just as a human would while trying to eliminate the possibility of human error. This could be from creating a robot capable of human emotions and empathy to something as mundane as the latest car model that is able to brake on its own when it senses danger. Artificial Intelligence takes many forms and as time goes on, more research is conducted in understanding the progression of this innovative concept.

Artificial intelligence is also a very controversial topic. After all, the idea of making a robot able to perform as a human may be a bit unsettling to many people. The controversy behind this also ties into the concept of “Free Will” that was discussed in lecture. It was often said that Free Will is a key trait that makes a human identifiable as a human. The definition of free will is the notion that one is free to choose his/her own actions regardless of any external factors. In other words, free will is simply the ability for humans to make decisions regardless of environmental influences.

Because of the technological advancements made in the past few years, I have found many articles regarding information on Artificial Intelligence. One news article that really stuck out was one that I found on bbc.com titled “Go master quits because AI ‘cannot be defeated’”. For a bit of background information, a while back I had watched a documentary on Netflix about the development of an Artificial Intelligence program called “AlphaGo” made to master the Chinese board game called “Go”. This program was supposed to be adaptive, meaning that it would learn and develop based on its wins and losses through playing the actual board game.

The “ultimate test” to see how advanced this program really is was to put it up against the world’s greatest Go player, Lee Se-dol, who had only ended up winning one out of the three matches against the Artificial Intelligence program.

This article by BBC news talks about how Lee Se-dol’s defeat against Artificial Intelligence program AlphaGo has caused him to retire from competition. In other words, the research conducted in the development of AlphaGo as well as the realization that Artificial Intelligence programs are much more advanced than he thought is what caused him to retire. The research conducted regarding AlphaGo was extensive and, rather than measuring activity of the human subject, it measured the activity and adaptability of the software program. This article goes into how it is difficult for an Artificial Intelligence program to determine whether it is winning or not because many of the top players rely on instinct to determine this for themselves (BBC). This shows how the research conducted on the program had to be complicated and extensive in order for it to perform at a level even higher than the top-rated players.

In relation to this, another article was found that went into depth in terms of the actual research that went into the development of AlphaGo itself. This article titled “Anthropomorphizing AlphaGO: a content analysis of the framing of Google DeepMind’s AlphaGo in the Chinese and American press” by Nathaniel Ming Curran, Jingyi Sun, and Joo-Wha Hong, talks about the background of AI, the controversy behind AI versus humans, and the interactions between AI and humans in the development of AlphaGo. During the development of AlphaGo, the developers of this software had to put the program up against human competitors for it to learn and adapt based on wins and losses. This expanded its knowledge in a way similar to humans practicing in competitions. The developers measured, analyzed, and observed AlphaGo’s responses and growth during its performance against players over time to see how it “grew” in the sense that it became much more capable of winning and competing than it was before.

In terms of the development and advancement of Artificial Intelligence, I believe that it is a wonderful and revolutionary breakthrough that should continue to be researched and developed. Of course, there is always a downside to the development of more thigs like this such as making humans feel more inferior, however I believe that if AI is developed in the right way, we can learn so much more about the world and we as humans can be able to do so many more things. I think that breakthroughs like this AlphaGo software are amazing because it shows us how computers aren’t just capable of processing things at a fast rate, but are able to do so much more like think similarly to a human and use probability as a parallel to human intuition.

Neuroscience Definitions

Artificial Intelligence: the ability of a computer or robot controlled by a computer to do tasks that are usually done by humans because they require human intelligence and discernment

Behavioral Neuroscience: the application of the principles of biology to the study of physiological, genetic, and developmental mechanisms of behavior in humans and other animals

Neuroprosthesis: any biomedical engineered device designed to be linked to the peripheral or central nervous system and enhance the cognitive, motor, or sensory abilities of an organism

Cellular Neuroscience: the study if science at a cellular level that includes cell morphology and physiological properties such as membrane trafficking, synthesis and transport of proteins, and synaptic plasticity

Cognitive Neuroscience: the scientific field that is concerned with the study of the biological processes and aspects that underlie cognition, with a specific focus on the neural connections I the brain that are involved in mental processes

Cognitive Science: the interdisciplinary, scientific study of the mind and its process that examines the nature, tasks, and functions of cognition in a broad sense

Computational Neuroscience: the study of the development, structure, physiology, information processing and cognitive abilities of the nervous system

Developmental Neuroscience: an interdisciplinary scientific field devoted to understanding physiological processes and their neurological bases in the developing organism

History of Neuroscience: the evidence of neuroscience practices throughout early periods of time

Neuroendocrinology: the branch of biology and physiology which studies the interaction between the nervous system and the endocrine system such as how the brain regulates the hormonal activity in the body

Molecular Neuroscience: a branch of neuroscience that examines the biology of the nervous system witch molecular biology, molecular genetics, protein chemistry and related methodologies

Neural Engineering: a discipline within biomedical engineering that uses engineering techniques to understand, repair, replace, or enhance neural systems

Neuroanatomy: the study of the structure and organization of the nervous system

Neurobiology: the study of cells of the nervous system and the organization of these cells into functional circuits that process information and mediate behavior

Neuroethology: the study of the neural basis of natural behavior in animals

Neuroimaging: a branch of medical imaging that focuses on the brain

Neurolinguistics: the branch of linguistics dealing with the relationship between language and the structure and functioning of the brain

Neurophysiology: the physiology of the nervous system

Neuropsychology: branch of psychology that is concerned with how a person’s cognition and behavior are related to the brain and the rest of the nervous system
